The Process

MC3 in order

Straight from the wallchart: Build Rapport and Listen with Purpose are Effective Communication Skills, applied immediately and ongoing in any interaction. Engage through Gain Commitment are Consultative Selling Skills. Objections and Disinterest are handled whenever they come up.

Build RapportDeveloping a relationship through interpersonal skills
Listen with PurposeFocusing attention to gather information and build relationships
EngageOpening the conversation
ExploreAsking questions to understand Needs and Motivators
EnableAddressing Needs and Motivators with Marine Corps features and benefits
Gain CommitmentObtaining the most aggressive commitment possible
Respond to ObjectionsRespond to objections in a way that resolves them
Respond to DisinterestGaining agreement to have a conversation with someone who is disinterested

Game 01Teams · Stations

MC3 Relay Race

Teach the full MC3 process in order.

Full ProcessReview
Rules & examples

How it works

Set up eight stations around the room, one per MC3 step: Build Rapport, Listen with Purpose, Engage, Explore, Enable, Gain Commitment, Respond to Objections, Respond to Disinterest. Teams race through in order. At each station they must answer a question, identify a "wrong turn," or perform a short roleplay before moving on.

Example challenge: At the Explore station, the Marine must ask an open question that uncovers a need or motivator.

Marines learn that MC3 is a process, not just random talking.

Game 021-on-1 · Cards

Wrong Turn Knockout

Help Marines recognize mistakes.

Wrong TurnsReview
Rules & examples

How it works

Create cards mixing the wallchart's HOW actions (correct moves) with its Wrong Turns. Marines compete one-on-one: the instructor reads a scenario, and each Marine must decide whether the card is a correct move or a wrong turn.

Wrong turn cards (exact wallchart wording)

  • Inappropriate words, humor or sexual references · Intimidation · Sounding robotic (Build Rapport)
  • Physical distractions · Personal biases · Snap judgments · Thinking ahead (Listen with Purpose)
  • Engaging without rapport (Engage)
  • Too many of one type of question (open or closed) · Blind/random questions · Talking more than asking (Explore)
  • Features without benefits · "Dumptrucking" irrelevant features and benefits (Enable)
  • Rehashing the whole conversation · Summarizing features without benefits · Asking for an easy commitment · Introducing new information (Gain Commitment)
  • Not understanding the objection before trying to respond to it · Trying to tip the scales with the wrong benefits · Arguing (Objections)
  • Letting disinterest end a conversation · Agreeing with disinterest · Ignoring disinterest (Disinterest)

Correct move cards

  • Demonstrate Corps Values · Find Common Ground · Match and Mirror
  • Listen for Facts and Feelings · Observe Body Language · Listen to Understand · Listen to Build Trust
  • Reflect Needs and Motivators · Align with the Need · Test for Yes
Example: "The applicant says they are not interested, and the recruiter keeps pushing harder." → Wrong turn — ignoring disinterest.

Marines learn what not to do during a conversation.

Game 03Pairs · Roleplay

Objection Boss Fight

Practice responding to objections.

Objections
Rules & examples

How it works

One Marine plays the "Boss," giving objections. The other Marine must defeat each objection using the wallchart's HOW, word for word:

  1. Ask Questions to Understand the Objection
  2. Respect the Objection
  3. Address the Objection:
    • Doubts it — prove it
    • Misunderstands it — explore the Need that exists
    • Doesn't want it or can't get it — tip the scales with priority benefits
  4. Test for Yes

Boss objections

  • "I need to talk to my parents."
  • "I'm not interested."
  • "I don't think I can pass boot camp."
  • "College is more important."
  • "I don't want to leave home."
Combat rules: The Boss has 3 health points. A good response removes 1 point. A poor response gives the Boss a counterattack. Bonus damage for naming the right Address path (doubt, misunderstanding, or doesn't want it/can't get it).

Marines practice resolving objections without the wallchart's wrong turns: not understanding the objection before trying to respond to it, trying to tip the scales with the wrong benefits, or arguing.

Game 04Pairs · Hidden cards

Motivator Detective

Teach Marines to identify needs and motivators.

ExploreListening
Rules & examples

How it works

The wallchart defines a motivator as a force that can drive a need: Circumstances (life situation, events, etc.), Feelings (what a person cares about, beliefs), Goals (Assets, Accomplishments, Appearance). One Marine receives a hidden motivator card — Money, Family pride, Education, Challenge, Travel, Purpose, Career direction, Belonging, Discipline, or Physical improvement — and the other must discover it using the Explore HOW: Use Questions to Explore Needs and Motivators, Reflect Needs and Motivators, Test for Yes.

Rules

No direct guessing ("Is your motivator money?"). Use open questions, which the wallchart says begin with phrases like "How do you feel about," "Why is that important," etc.:

  • "What are you hoping to change in your life?"
  • "What matters most to you after high school?"
  • "Why is that important to you?"

Motivators can be circumstances, feelings, or goals — and Explore's questions find them.

Game 05Teams · Matching

Feature or Benefit?

Teach the difference between Marine Corps features and benefits.

Enable
Rules & examples

How it works

Start with the wallchart definitions, word for word: a feature is an identifiable characteristic of an organization or product; a benefit is the specific value a feature provides. Create two piles of cards — Features and Benefits. Teams must match features to the correct applicant need.

Sample pairs

Feature: "Marine Corps offers education benefits."
Benefit: "That can help you pay for college and reduce financial stress."
Feature: "Marines receive leadership training."
Benefit: "That can help you build confidence and become more competitive in future careers."

The Enable HOW: Align with the Need, Demonstrate How Relevant Features and Benefits Meet the Need, Test for Yes. The wrong turns are features without benefits and "dumptrucking" irrelevant features and benefits.

Game 06Rotating pairs · 2 min

Rapport Speed Round

Practice building rapport quickly.

Rapport
Rules & examples

How it works

Marines rotate partners every two minutes. Each pair must build rapport using one of the wallchart's HOW methods: Demonstrate Corps Values, Find Common Ground, or Match and Mirror.

Scoring (by partner)

  • Did they sound natural?
  • Did they find common ground?
  • Did they avoid the wrong turns — inappropriate words, humor or sexual references; intimidation; sounding robotic?

Build Rapport is developing a relationship through interpersonal skills — to facilitate trust and an honest exchange of information, immediately and ongoing in any interaction.

Game 07Pairs · Acting

Listen With Purpose Charades

Teach active listening and body language.

Listening
Rules & examples

How it works

One Marine acts out a feeling without saying it: nervous, excited, unsure, defensive, curious, pressured, or disinterested. The other Marine must identify the feeling and respond using the wallchart's HOW: Listen to Understand (clarify and confirm), Listen for Facts and Feelings (listen for both), Observe Body Language (look for clues about feelings), and Listen to Build Trust (listen actively).

Example response: "You seem unsure about something. What part of this are you most concerned about?"

Listen with Purpose is focusing attention to gather information and build relationships. Its wrong turns: physical distractions, personal biases, snap judgments, and thinking ahead.

Game 08Scenarios · Decision

Gain Commitment Ladder

Practice asking for the most aggressive realistic commitment.

Commitment
Rules & examples

How it works

The wallchart's WHAT: obtaining the most aggressive commitment possible. Give Marines applicant scenarios. They must run the HOW word for word — Summarize Priority Benefits, Ask for a Commitment, State Next Steps, Test for Yes — choosing the strongest realistic commitment.

Commitment options

  • Set an appointment
  • Complete an application
  • Bring parents to the office
  • Take a practice ASVAB
  • Speak with an influencer
  • Follow up next week
  • Review information online
Example: "Based on what you said about wanting structure and career direction, the next step is to come by the office tomorrow at 1600 so we can go over your options. Does that work?"
Follow-through: After Test for Yes, the wallchart branches to Maintain Commitment (Provide Relevant Collateral; Refer to Websites and Other Information; Prepare the Applicant to Speak to Influencers) or Respond to Refusal (Ask Questions to Understand the Refusal; Restart the Conversation if Appropriate; Respect the Refusal).

Avoid the wrong turns: rehashing the whole conversation, summarizing features without benefits, asking for an easy commitment, and introducing new information.

Game 10Groups · Roleplay

Recruiter Scenario Cards

Apply the whole process in realistic conversations.

Full ProcessObjectionsDisinterest
Rules & examples

How it works

Each group draws a scenario card and roleplays the conversation through all six steps — Build Rapport, Listen with Purpose, Engage, Explore, Enable, Gain Commitment. If an objection or disinterest appears, they must respond correctly.

Sample cards

  • A high school senior wants college but thinks the Marine Corps will stop them from going.
  • An applicant is physically fit but says their parents do not support the military.
  • A student says they are not interested and only stopped by because their friend did.
  • A graduate wants travel, money, and purpose but does not know much about the Marine Corps.

This turns the wallchart into real practice.

Game 11Pairs · Roleplay

Disinterest Recovery Game

Practice responding to someone who does not appear interested.

Disinterest
Rules & examples

How it works

One Marine opens every scenario with disinterest: "I'm good." · "I don't want to talk." · "The military isn't for me." · "I'm just waiting for my friend." · "I already have plans."

The recovery process (exact wallchart HOW)

  1. Ask Questions to Understand the Disinterest — identify the End State (Circumstances, Feelings, Goals)
  2. State the Value of Continuing the Conversation (Value or Importance) — Test for Yes
  3. Exploring Disinterest — Use Questions to Uncover Needs and Motivators; Reflect Needs and Motivators (Use Future Impact Questions to heighten awareness); Test for Yes
  4. State Next Steps (Most Aggressive Commitment Possible) — Set the Appointment, or Build Applicant Profile/Follow Up
  5. Respect the Disinterest

The wallchart's wrong turns: letting disinterest end a conversation, agreeing with disinterest, and ignoring disinterest.

Game 12Class · Card hands

MC3 Card Battle

Make the concepts competitive.

ReviewFull Process
Rules & examples

How it works

Each Marine holds a hand of skill cards using the wallchart's exact terms: Open Question, Closed Question, Build Rapport, Listen for Facts and Feelings, Reflect Needs and Motivators, Align with the Need, Test for Yes, Respect the Objection, Wrong Turn, Gain Commitment. The instructor reads a scenario; Marines play the best card and explain why. Best answer wins the round.

Example scenario: "My parents would never let me join." → Best plays: Ask Questions to Understand the Objection · Respect the Objection · Address the Objection (misunderstands it — explore the Need that exists) · Test for Yes.

Marines learn which skill fits which part of the conversation.
