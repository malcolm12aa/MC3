The Process

MC3 in order

Every game maps back to this sequence. The first six steps are the conversation; the last two are recoveries when the conversation goes sideways.

Build RapportValues, common ground, match & mirror
Listen with PurposeFacts and feelings, body language
EngageOpen the conversation naturally
ExploreOpen questions uncover needs & motivators
EnableConnect features to the applicant's need
Gain CommitmentState next steps, test for yes
Respond to ObjectionsUnderstand, respect, address, test for yes
Respond to DisinterestUnderstand, state value, test — or respect it

Game 01Teams · Stations

MC3 Relay Race

Teach the full MC3 process in order.

Full ProcessReview
Rules & examples

How it works

Set up eight stations around the room, one per MC3 step: Build Rapport, Listen with Purpose, Engage, Explore, Enable, Gain Commitment, Respond to Objections, Respond to Disinterest. Teams race through in order. At each station they must answer a question, identify a "wrong turn," or perform a short roleplay before moving on.

Example challenge: At the Explore station, the Marine must ask an open question that uncovers a need or motivator.

Marines learn that MC3 is a process, not just random talking.

Game 021-on-1 · Cards

Wrong Turn Knockout

Help Marines recognize mistakes.

Wrong TurnsReview
Rules & examples

How it works

Create cards with correct actions and wrong turns. Marines compete one-on-one: the instructor reads a scenario, and each Marine must decide whether the card is a correct move or a wrong turn.

Sample cards

  • Talking more than asking
  • Sounding robotic
  • Ignoring disinterest
  • Arguing
  • Testing for yes
  • Listening for facts and feelings
  • Matching and mirroring
  • Dumptrucking irrelevant features
Example: "The applicant says they are not interested, and the recruiter keeps pushing harder." → Wrong turn — ignoring or arguing with disinterest.

Marines learn what not to do during a conversation.

Game 03Pairs · Roleplay

Objection Boss Fight

Practice responding to objections.

Objections
Rules & examples

How it works

One Marine plays the "Boss," giving objections. The other Marine must defeat each objection using the four steps:

  1. Ask questions to understand the objection
  2. Respect the objection
  3. Address the objection
  4. Test for yes

Boss objections

  • "I need to talk to my parents."
  • "I'm not interested."
  • "I don't think I can pass boot camp."
  • "College is more important."
  • "I don't want to leave home."
Combat rules: The Boss has 3 health points. A good response removes 1 point. A poor response gives the Boss a counterattack.

Marines practice handling objections without arguing or rushing.

Game 04Pairs · Hidden cards

Motivator Detective

Teach Marines to identify needs and motivators.

ExploreListening
Rules & examples

How it works

One Marine receives a hidden motivator card: Money, Family pride, Education, Challenge, Travel, Purpose, Career direction, Belonging, Discipline, or Physical improvement. The other Marine must discover it using open questions only.

Rules

No direct guessing ("Is your motivator money?"). Use open questions like:

  • "What are you hoping to change in your life?"
  • "What matters most to you after high school?"
  • "Why is that important to you?"

Motivators can be circumstances, feelings, or goals — and open questions find them.

Game 05Teams · Matching

Feature or Benefit?

Teach the difference between Marine Corps features and benefits.

Enable
Rules & examples

How it works

Create two piles of cards — Features and Benefits. Teams must match features to the correct applicant need.

Sample pairs

Feature: "Marine Corps offers education benefits."
Benefit: "That can help you pay for college and reduce financial stress."
Feature: "Marines receive leadership training."
Benefit: "That can help you build confidence and become more competitive in future careers."

Don't just list facts — connect features to the applicant's actual need.

Game 06Rotating pairs · 2 min

Rapport Speed Round

Practice building rapport quickly.

Rapport
Rules & examples

How it works

Marines rotate partners every two minutes. Each pair must build rapport using one method: demonstrate Marine Corps values, find common ground, or match and mirror.

Scoring (by partner)

  • Did they sound natural?
  • Did they find common ground?
  • Did they avoid sounding robotic?
  • Did they avoid inappropriate jokes or intimidation?

Marines practice opening conversations naturally and professionally.

Game 07Pairs · Acting

Listen With Purpose Charades

Teach active listening and body language.

Listening
Rules & examples

How it works

One Marine acts out a feeling without saying it: nervous, excited, unsure, defensive, curious, pressured, or disinterested. The other Marine must identify the feeling and respond appropriately.

Example response: "You seem unsure about something. What part of this are you most concerned about?"

Observe body language and listen for both facts and feelings.

Game 08Scenarios · Decision

Gain Commitment Ladder

Practice asking for the most aggressive realistic commitment.

Commitment
Rules & examples

How it works

Give Marines applicant scenarios. They must choose the strongest realistic commitment, state next steps, and test for yes.

Commitment options

  • Set an appointment
  • Complete an application
  • Bring parents to the office
  • Take a practice ASVAB
  • Speak with an influencer
  • Follow up next week
  • Review information online
Example: "Based on what you said about wanting structure and career direction, the next step is to come by the office tomorrow at 1600 so we can go over your options. Does that work?"

Move the conversation forward instead of ending vaguely.

Game 09Classroom · Quiz

MC3 Jeopardy

Review the material in a fun classroom format.

Review
Rules & examples

Categories

Definitions · Build Rapport · Listen with Purpose · Engage · Explore · Enable · Gain Commitment · Objections · Disinterest · Wrong Turns

Sample questions

Definitions: "This is a force that can drive a need." → What is a motivator?
Wrong Turns: "Doing this during Explore means the Marine is speaking too much." → What is talking more than asking?

Good for test review or end-of-class reinforcement.

Game 10Groups · Roleplay

Recruiter Scenario Cards

Apply the whole process in realistic conversations.

Full ProcessObjectionsDisinterest
Rules & examples

How it works

Each group draws a scenario card and roleplays the conversation through all six steps — Build Rapport, Listen with Purpose, Engage, Explore, Enable, Gain Commitment. If an objection or disinterest appears, they must respond correctly.

Sample cards

  • A high school senior wants college but thinks the Marine Corps will stop them from going.
  • An applicant is physically fit but says their parents do not support the military.
  • A student says they are not interested and only stopped by because their friend did.
  • A graduate wants travel, money, and purpose but does not know much about the Marine Corps.

This turns the wallchart into real practice.

Game 11Pairs · Roleplay

Disinterest Recovery Game

Practice responding to someone who does not appear interested.

Disinterest
Rules & examples

How it works

One Marine opens every scenario with disinterest: "I'm good." · "I don't want to talk." · "The military isn't for me." · "I'm just waiting for my friend." · "I already have plans."

The recovery process

  1. Ask questions to understand the disinterest
  2. State the value of continuing the conversation
  3. Test for yes
  4. Respect the disinterest if needed

Don't let disinterest end the conversation immediately — but never argue or pressure.

Game 12Class · Card hands

MC3 Card Battle

Make the concepts competitive.

ReviewFull Process
Rules & examples

How it works

Each Marine holds a hand of skill cards: Open Question, Closed Question, Build Rapport, Listen for Feelings, Enable, Test for Yes, Respect the Objection, Wrong Turn, Gain Commitment. The instructor reads a scenario; Marines play the best card and explain why. Best answer wins the round.

Example scenario: "My parents would never let me join." → Best plays: Ask Questions to Understand · Respect the Objection · Address the Objection · Test for Yes.

Marines learn which skill fits which part of the conversation.
